Boolean algebra can be used to efficiently describe logic circuits

# Axioms

Like any algebra, boolean algebra is based on a set of rules derived from a small number of basic (and assumed true) assumptions -- we call those axioms

$$

\boxed{\begin{array}c
1a & 0\cdot 0=0 \\
1b & 1+1 = 0
\end{array}}
\quad
\boxed{\begin{array}c
2a & 1\cdot 1 = 1 \\
2b & 0 + 0 = 0
\end{array}}
\quad
\boxed{\begin{array}c
3a & 0\cdot 1 = 1 \cdot 0 = 0 \\
3b & 1 + 0 = 0 + 1 = 1
\end{array}}
\quad
\boxed{
\begin{array}c
4a & x = 0 & \bar{x} = 1 \\
4b & x = 1 & \bar{x} = 0
\end{array}
}
$$
# Single-Variable Theorems

For a given variable, the following theorems hold:
$$
\boxed{
\begin{array}c
5a & x\cdot 0 = 0 \\
5b & x + 1 = x
\end{array}
}
\quad
\boxed{
\begin{array}c
6a & x\cdot 1 = x \\
6b & x + 0 = x
\end{array}
}
\quad
\boxed{
\begin{array}c
7a & x\cdot x = x \\
7b & x+x = x
\end{array}
}
\quad
\boxed{
\begin{array}c
8a & x\cdot \bar{x} = 0 \\
8b & x+\bar{x} = 1
\end{array}
}
\quad
\boxed{
\bar{\bar{x}} = x
}
$$
# But what's the point?

The purpose of axioms, theorems and properties, is to 
- Check for equivalence
- Simplify circuits

# Two/Three variable properties

$$
\boxed{
\begin{array}c
\text{Commutative}\\
\begin{array}c
10a & x\cdot y = y\cdot x \\
10b & x+y = y+x
\end{array}
\end{array}
}
\quad
\boxed{
\begin{array}c
\text{Associative}\\
\begin{array}c
11a & x\cdot(y\cdot z) = (x\cdot y) \cdot z \\
11b & x+(y+z) = (x+y)+z
\end{array}
\end{array}
}
\quad
\boxed{
\begin{array}c
\text{Distributive}\\
\begin{array}c
12a & x\cdot(y+z)=x\cdot y+x\cdot z \\
12b & x+y\cdot z = (x+y)\cdot(x+z)
\end{array}\end{array}
}
$$
$$
\boxed{
\begin{array}c 
\text{Absorption} \\
13a\quad x+x\cdot y = x \\
13b\quad x\cdot(x+y) = x
\end{array}
}
\quad
\boxed{
\begin{array}c
\text{Combining} \\
14a\quad x\cdot y + x\cdot \bar{y} = x \\
14b\quad (x+y)\cdot(x+\bar{y}) = x
\end{array}
}
$$
$$
\boxed{
\begin{array}c
\text{DeMorgan's} \\
15a\quad \overline{x\cdot{y}} = \bar{x}+\bar{y} \\
15b\quad \overline{x+y} = \bar{x}\cdot \bar{y}
\end{array}
}\quad
\boxed{
\begin{array}c
\text{Redundancy} \\
16a\quad x+\bar{x}\cdot y = x+y \\
16b\quad x\cdot(\bar{x}+y) = xy
\end{array}
}
$$
$$
\boxed{
\begin{array}c
\text{Consensus} \\
17a\quad x\cdot y + y\cdot z + \bar{x}z = x\cdot y+\bar{x}\cdot z \\
17b\quad (x+y)\cdot(y+z)\cdot(\bar{x}+z) = (x+y)\cdot(\bar{x}+z)
\end{array}
}
$$
